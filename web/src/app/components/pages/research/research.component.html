PREMISE

When the four of us sat down together our main goal was to come up with a topic that interested us all. A common theme among the things we talked about was self-love. Self-love is important to value yourself, have healthy boundaries with others, accept your personal failures and disappointments, be an advocate for yourself, and express who you are. Our intention from the start was to do an on campus intervention in the form of a collabrative art installation. Our traget audience was anyone who encountered our project, but mostly VCU students.

In our world today people are increasingly hard on themselves. In college it is easy to get wrapped up in work and people quickly forget to take time for themselves and relax. Our goal was to bring awareness to this, we wanted to make people stop and think about the good things in their life and even think into their future goals. We wanted it to be a repetitive action, something that gained more and more momentum over a series of days. Especially because a self-love centered mindset takes time to create.

Kintsugi

Japanese art of fixing broken vessels with a special lacquer that is then dusted with gold, silver or platinum. The practice is related to the japanese philosophy of wabi-sabi, which calls for seeing beauty in the flawed or imperfect. Imperfect but valuable, priceless, beautiful, worthy. This practice dates back to the 15th century. According to legend, the craft was created when Japanese shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa sent his cracked chawan, tea bowl, back to China to undergo repairs. When it was returned to him, Yoshimasa was displeased to see that it was mended with unsightly metal staples. This encouraged craftsmen to find an alternative, a more aesthetically pleasing method of repair.

There are 3 predominant styles of Kintsugi.

Crack Method

Objects mended using this method are pieced back together and secured with minimal lacquer.

Piece Method

Objects restored with this method feature replacement fragments made entirely of epoxy.

Joint Call

Objects fixed with this method employ similarly shaped pieces from other broken wares.

Tanabata Wishing Tree

The Tanabata Festival originated from the ancient Chinese legend known as Qixi. It is a story of two lovers, Princess Orihime (a seamstress and Hikoboshi (a cow herder). Princess Orihime was also the daughter of Tentei, the God of the Heavens. She wove beautiful clothes which represented the Milky Way. Her father arranged the first meeting of Princess Orihime and Hikoboshi to end the sadness and despair of his daughter. Princess Orihime and Hikoboshi instantly fell in love with each other. They were so in love that they began to neglect their work. The God of the Heavens was angered by the both of them and forbade them to be together. After pleading and promising to weave once again, Orihime was granted permission to meet Hikoboshi again, but the two star-crossed lovers could only meet once a year. Hikoboshi is known as the Cowherd Star (Altair) and Orihime as the Weaver Star (Vega). The two lovers Are separated by the river (Milky Way). Every 7th of July, a bridge will be made by magpies for Orihime as long as the sky is clear.

On Tanabata people write wishes (sometimes in the form of poetry) on small pieces of paper called tanzaku and hang them on bamboo trees. These slips of paper may also be hung with other decorations. The following day the trees are floated on a river and burned as an offering.
